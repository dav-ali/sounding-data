Sounding Data

How does
wellness sound?

An ongoing research project exploring whether the felt experience of everyday behavior can be made audible through sonification.

Scroll
The Question

Screen-time is just a number.
What matters is what it feels like.

"What began as a simple curiosity — can a phone log sound like anything? — grew into a question about whether musical deformation could carry the weight of lived experience."

Screen-time data is a number: hours and minutes recorded by a device. But the number is not the experience. Research has shown that heavy screen use tends to co-occur with specific bodily and affective states — restlessness, mental fatigue, anxiety, a sense of time slipping away.

This project asks: can those states be made audible? Two approaches were explored in parallel: deformation — taking a familiar piece and letting it drift from what it should sound like — and generative composition — building music from scratch using the data itself as compositional material. Each approach embeds a different theory of what sonification is for.

The deformation approach rests on a simple premise: any sound deliberately composed already sounds "right." The only way to represent deviation from wellness is to take something healthy and let it slip. The deformation is the representation.

The Journey

From curiosity
to working systems.

Origin
A question about a phone log
The project began with a simple provocation: if a day of screen-time data were mapped onto sound, would it mean anything to a listener? The first experiments were command-line scripts generating music from three activity levels — low, medium, high.
System I — Generative v1
Building music from data: neutral
Three layers — a sustained pad, a rhythmic pluck, a synth bass — responded to hourly intensity values. Activity level controlled chord density, register, rhythmic density, and velocity. All in C minor pentatonic — deliberately neutral in its sonic ideology.
System II — Generative v2
Building music from data: with a value judgment
The same generative engine, but with a key change: the scale itself shifted with intensity. Major pentatonic for low activity (bright, open, resolved), minor pentatonic for medium (darker, weighted), chromatic clusters for high (tense, unstable, dissonant). The sonic language now carried a value judgment about the data.
System III — Transformation
Deforming what already exists
Rather than building music from data, this approach took existing music and deformed it. Beethoven's Piano Sonata No. 25, third movement — vivace, simple, familiar — was chosen because listeners already know what it should sound like. Three independent deformation parameters: pitch instability, rhythmic jitter, timbral degradation.
System IV — Full Day
A whole day, rendered in sound
The transformation system was extended to work with real screen-time data — hourly readings from UVA's Link Lab. For any given day, the system generates a continuous sonification: Beethoven deformed hour by hour according to actual usage, with a synchronized video showing the day's bar chart moving in time with the music.
System V — Interactive
The listener becomes a participant
The most recent system puts the listener in control. Sliders set the intensity for each hour of a 16-hour day. The user chooses which deformation parameters respond — and whether the sonic language carries a value judgment (major → minor → chromatic) or remains neutral.
Now — Perception Study
Asking what listeners actually hear
An ongoing study asks participants to name the states they associate with high screen-time, then evaluate whether each deformation parameter communicates those states. A parallel group evaluates the generative approach. The goal: understand which sonic dimensions carry which affective meanings — and whether deformation or generation communicates more reliably.

Approach II — Generative

Music built
from the data itself.

Rather than deforming an existing piece, the generative system constructs music from scratch using screen-time intensity as compositional material. Three instrumental layers — a sustained pad, a rhythmic pluck, and a synth bass — respond to hourly activity values. What changes with intensity is not just loudness or density, but the harmonic language itself.

The three harmonic modes

Low screen-time generates music in major pentatonic — open, resolved, bright. Medium generates minor pentatonic — darker, weighted, ambivalent. High intensity produces chromatic clusters — tense, dissonant, unstable. The choice of scale is itself an ideological act: the system encodes a value judgment about what high screen-time means.

Low intensity — Major pentatonic
A settled day
Open, bright, resolved. Low chord density, sparse rhythm, soft bass. A day with space in it.
0:00
Mid intensity — Minor pentatonic
A weighted day
Darker, denser. The chord fills in, the rhythm quickens, the bass becomes more present. Something is accumulating.
0:00
High intensity — Chromatic
A saturated day
Dissonant cluster chords, dense rapid figures, aggressive bass. The system has moved outside conventional harmony entirely.

The Study

Can listeners
hear the difference?

An ongoing perception study asks whether each deformation parameter communicates specific affective states — and whether what listeners hear depends on what they personally associate with high screen-time.

Phase 1
Personal mapping
Research has shown that heavy screen use co-occurs with specific bodily states. Participants select three that resonate with their own experience: restlessness, mental fatigue, anxiety, emptiness, time-loss, sleep disturbance.
Phase 2
Listening task
Thirteen musical excerpts, each deforming the Beethoven piece through one or more parameters at varying intensities. After each, participants rate the three states they chose — and how strongly the excerpt evokes a high screen-time day.
Phase 3
Reflection
Participants reflect on whether listening gave them a different kind of insight than looking at a graph would have. Open-text responses probe what the music communicated that numbers could not.
Parallel arm
Two approaches compared
A second group evaluates music generated from scratch using an algorithmic system by Miya. Same states, same sliders — asking whether familiarity-with-deviation carries meaning differently than generation from nothing.
